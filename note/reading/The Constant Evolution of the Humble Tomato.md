**The Constant Evolution of the Humble Tomato**

*Heirloom tomatoes—varieties that have been passed down through several generations of a family because they are thought to have a particularly good flavor—are really no more “natural” than the varieties available in grocery stores. New studies promise to restore their lost, healthy genes.*

**A** Famous for their taste, color, and organic appearance, heirloom tomatoes are favorites of gardeners and advocates of locally grown foods. [[The tomato enthusiast might conclude that, given the immense varieties, heirlooms must have a more diverse and superior set of genes than the tomatoes available in grocery stores... However, their seeming diversity is only %%skin-deep%%: heirlooms are actually %%feeble and inbred%%]]—the defective product of breeding experiments... “The irony of all this,” says **Steven Tanksley**, a geneticist at Cornell University, [[“is that all that diversity of heirlooms can be accounted for by a %%handful of genes%%. There are probably %%no more than 10 mutant genes%% that create the diversity of heirlooms you see.”]] But rather than simply proving that the %%myth%% about the heirlooms’ diversity is wrong, Tanksley’s deconstruction of the tomato genome... is showing how a small berry-like fruit from the Andes became one of the world’s top crops.

**B** The cultivated tomato is a member of the nightshade family that includes New World crops such as the potato, which spread around the globe after Christopher Columbus brought them back to Spain in the 15th century. [[But whereas scientists have uncovered a %%wealth of archaeological evidence%% on early farming practices in the New World, the %%record is blank%% when it comes to the tomato.]] [[The modern tomato seems to have its %%origins in the Andes%% in South America]] and may have been domesticated in Veracruz, Mexico. Primitive varieties still grow throughout the Americas. All told, botanists call as many as 13 species “tomatoes” and consider an additional four to be closely related.

**C** One might assume that one of these known wild species became today’s cultivated crop, but that’s not the case: the Mother Tomato has never been found. [[The %%closest relative%% is the %%currant%% tomato]], which, based on genetic comparisons, split from today’s tomato some 1.4 million years ago. So researchers like Tanksley have to work backward, crossing tomato varieties and species... Once isolated, Tanksley later inserts those genes into other tomato varieties to make his case with a dramatic transformation.

**D** Tanksley concludes from his analyses that in their effort to make bigger, tastier, and faster-growing fruit, our ancestors ultimately exploited just 30 mutations out of the tomato’s 35,000 genes. Most of these genes have only small effects on tomato size and shape, [[but recently Tanksley and his colleagues reported that they %%found a gene that increases fruit size by 50 percent%%. It was probably the %%most important event in domestication%%.]] The first written record of tomatoes—from Spain in the 1500s—confirms that this %%mutation%%, which enlarges tomatoes by producing compartments known as locules, existed back in the same yellow tomatoes that gave Italians the word %%pomodoro%%, or golden apple. [[Besides size, tomato farmers also selected for shape. To discover those genes, **Esther van der Knaap**... took a gene from one heirloom tomato and %%inserted it into a wild relative%%. She observed that, as a result, the tiny fruits %%became shaped like pears%%.]]

**E** The selection of these traits has, however, affected the heirlooms’ hardiness. [[They often suffer from %%infections%% that cause the fruit to %%crack, split, and otherwise rot quickly%%.]] Wild plants must continuously evolve to fend off such infections, points out **Roger Chetelat**... But in their quest for size, shape, and flavor, humans have inadvertently eliminated defensive genes. [[**Chetelat** elaborates that %%heirlooms’ taste may have less to do with their genes than with the productivity of the plant and the growing environment%%.]] [[Any plant that produces only two fruits, as heirlooms sometimes do, is highly likely to produce %%juicier, sweeter, and more flavorful%% fruit than varieties that produce 100...]] This means breeders feel confident that getting germ-beating genes back into heirlooms won’t harm the desirable aspects of the fruit. Modern breeding has resuscitated grocery-store tomatoes with an influx of wild genes...

**F** [[In 1996, a tomato breeder and former Tanksley student named **Doug Heath** began a favorite project... he created a %%new multi-colored tomato less prone to cracking and also endowed with 12 disease-resistant genes%%.]] The original heirloom plant, Heath explains, had defective flowers, which is one reason why it produced only two fruits compared with the 30 he gets from his new variety. [[He claims he is also able to %%maintain a comparable flavor and sugar profile%% even on productive plants.]] The heirloom’s defects are, after all, just an accident of a narrow breeding strategy left over from the very beginning of genetic modification.

**Questions 14–17 (配对题)**

**Q14: an explanation of research aimed at restoring the health of the heirloom tomato**
- 定位词：restoring the health, heirloom
- 原文：F段 ...he created a **new... tomato less prone to cracking and also endowed with 12 disease-resistant genes**.
- 分析：F段详述了 Doug Heath 如何通过育种让传家宝番茄更健康（抗裂、增加抗病基因），即“旨在恢复健康的科研”。
- ✅ 答案：**F**

**Q15: a reference to a false belief about the heirloom tomato**
- 定位词：false belief, heirloom
- 原文：A段 ...the **myth** about the heirlooms’ diversity is wrong... heirlooms are actually **feeble and inbred**
- 分析：原文提到人们认为传家宝番茄基因更多样（immense varieties），但实际上这种多样性只是“皮毛”（skin-deep），是误解（myth）。
- ✅ 答案：**A**

- 分析：E段具体描述了传家宝番茄的味道特征（更多汁、更甜、更有风味）。
- ✅ 答案：**E**

**Q17: a reference to a single gene that significantly improves the cultivation of tomatoes**
- 定位词：single gene, significantly improves

**Q18: The transplanting of certain genes into tomatoes can change their shape.**

### 句一：

**原句**：
They often suffer from ==infections== that cause the fruit to ==crack, split, and otherwise rot quickly==.

**单词**：
1. infection n.感染
2. crack v.破裂  n.裂缝
3. split v.分裂
4. rot v.腐败 n.腐烂物
---
### 句二：

**原句：**
Wild plants must continuously evolve to fend off such infections, points out Roger Chetelat of the Tomato Genetics Resource Center at the University of California. 

**结构拆解:**
- **[引述内容]**: "Wild plants must continuously evolve..." (作为 points out 的逻辑宾语)
- **谓语 (Verb)**: points out (指出)
- **主语 (Subject)**: Roger Chetelat (加上后面一长串的头衔/机构名)

**为什么倒装?**
- **避免头重脚轻 (End-weight Principle):** 当主语带有很长的修饰成分时，将动词提前可以使句子结构更平衡，读者能更快抓住“谁做了什么”。
- **新闻/学术风格:** 这种先说观点、后交代出处的写法在正式文体中非常普遍。

"...," said the spokesperson. （发言人说……）
“……”，发言人说道。（发言人说……）

"...," argues the scientist. （科学家辩称……）
“……”，科学家如此论述。（科学家辩称……）

"...," notes the author. （作者指出……）
“……”，作者指出。（作者写道……）

**词汇**：
- fend off ：抵御 
---
### 句三：

**原句**：
 But in their ==quest== for size, shape, and flavor, humans have ==inadvertently== ==eliminated defensive== genes.

**词汇**：
1. inadvertently
```Advertise (广告/宣传)
ad- (向/往) + vert (转) + -ise (动词后缀)
字面意思： 使人们的注意力“转向”某个东西。
逻辑： 所谓“打广告”，就是为了让你转过头来看这个产品。
```
```
Inadvertently (不经意地/非故意地)
in- (不) + ad- (向) + vert (转) + -ent (形容词后缀) + -ly (副词后缀)
字面意思： 你的心没有“转向”那一处。
逻辑： 当你做某事的时候，你的注意力并没有“转向”那个动作，也就是你没留神、没注意。
结果： 所以这个词翻译为“不经意地”或“无心之举”。
```
2. quest n.寻求
3. eliminate v.剔除，根除
